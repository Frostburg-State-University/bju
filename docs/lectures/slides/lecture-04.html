Part OneCash flow diagrams 03
Part — One
I

Before you compute a cash flow,
you should be able to draw it.

"The cash flow diagram is to the financial engineer what the free-body diagram is to the mechanical one."
— after Park, Contemporary Engineering Economics

§ I · DiagramsLecture Four
Set the analogy hard. In mechanics, no engineer would solve a statics problem without first drawing the free-body diagram. In financial engineering, the cash-flow diagram serves the same role: it forces you to make every assumption visible — sign, timing, magnitude — before you ever reach for a formula. ~1 minute, then move on.
§ IWhat & why 04
Working definition

A cash flow diagram is a picture of money in time.

A cash flow diagram (CFD) plots every receipt and every payment of a project on a single time axis, distinguishing each by sign, magnitude, and timing.

It is the bridge between a narrative ("we'll pay $100K up front, then receive $40K a year for four years") and a computation (PV, FV, NPV, IRR).

A CFD does not solve the problem. It makes the problem solvable by forcing every ambiguity onto the page.

It records, for each cash flow:

  • When it occurs — end of which period.
  • How much — in absolute terms.
  • Direction — into or out of the project.
  • Whose perspective — buyer, seller, lender, borrower.

Change perspective, and every arrow flips.

§ I · Diagrams04 / 31
Stress the last bullet — perspective. The same contract is a stream of outflows to the customer and inflows to the vendor. Many student mistakes come from drawing one CFD then quietly using it from the opposite perspective halfway through the problem. Anecdote: a student last summer computed the IRR of a SaaS contract from the vendor's perspective, then concluded the *customer* should sign it because the IRR was high. That confusion lives in the CFD; labelling perspective explicitly is the prophylactic. ~3 minutes.
§ IConventions 05
Four conventions to memorise

The grammar of the diagram.

ConventionMeaningWhy it matters
Horizontal axis Time, in discrete periods 0, 1, 2, … , n. Period 0 is "now." Forces you to choose a period (month vs. year); all rates must match it.
Arrows up Cash into the decision-maker (revenue, savings, salvage). Visual sign convention — green / positive.
Arrows down Cash out (cost, payment, investment). Visual sign convention — red / negative.
End-of-period rule Unless stated otherwise, every cash flow occurs at the end of its period. An "annual" cash flow at year 1 means month 12, not month 0.

Continuous and beginning-of-period conventions exist (annuity-due, continuous compounding) — we will name them when we need them.

§ I · Diagrams05 / 31
Hammer the end-of-period rule — it is the single most violated convention in homework. If a textbook says "annual maintenance of $5K starting in year 1," that money lands at t=1 (end of year 1), not at t=0. Most P/A, F/A formulas assume this; using a different timing without adjusting the formula gives wrong answers by a factor of (1+i). Tell them: when in doubt, label the diagram timing explicitly and check it matches the factor's assumption. ~3 minutes.
§ IReading a diagram 06
A first picture

A small software project, drawn in full.

$100K invested up-front; $40K of net cash flow returned each year for four years. From the investor's perspective.

Cash flow diagram · investor's perspective · annual periods

Naive total:  −100 + 4·40 = +$60K — looks like a win.

At i = 10%:  PV of inflows = $126.79K; PV of outflow = $100K; NPV ≈ +$26.79K.

§ I · Diagrams06 / 31
Walk the diagram element by element. Point to: (1) the time axis, (2) the single down-arrow at t=0 for the $100K investment, (3) the four equal up-arrows at t=1..4 for the $40K returns. Stress the end-of-period convention — those $40K arrows are at the END of each year. Show the gap between the "naive total" (+$60K) and the proper NPV (~$26.79K) — we will compute the latter formally in Lecture 5. Right now the point is just that the diagram makes the SHAPE visible: one down, four ups, equal magnitude. That shape has a name — it's a uniform series. Foreshadow Part II. ~4 minutes.
§ ICommon pitfalls 07
What goes wrong

Five mistakes that quietly invalidate a CFD.

  1. Mixing perspectives mid-problem — half the arrows are the customer's, half the vendor's.
  2. Mixing periods — monthly cash flow on a yearly rate. The factor will silently lie.
  3. Including sunk costs — past payments belong in history, not in the diagram.
  1. Ignoring opportunity cost — using your own time "for free" because no cash changes hands.
  2. Mis-timing the first cash flow — an annuity-due at t = 0 looks the same on paper as an ordinary annuity at t = 1, but they differ by a factor of (1+i).
When in doubt: write the perspective and the period at the top of the diagram. Two extra words save a wrong answer.
§ I · Diagrams07 / 31
Quick check-in: ask the class which of these five they have personally seen in a project. Sunk costs (#3) almost always gets the most hands — "we have already spent $200K, so we should finish" is the canonical sunk-cost fallacy. Remind them that the CFD is a *forward-looking* tool; the past goes in the post-mortem, not in the diagram. ~3 minutes.

Mortgages, leases, salaries, SaaS subscriptions — the world is full of uniform series.

§ II · Uniform seriesLecture Four
Brief transition. The uniform series is the workhorse of personal finance and of operating contracts. If the class only learns ONE family of factors today, this is the family. ~30 seconds.
§ IIFour factors 09
A is the constant cash flow at each period 1 … n

Four directions, four factors.

FactorReads asFormulaUsed to
P / A Present value of a uniform series. \( P = A \cdot \dfrac{(1+i)^n - 1}{i\,(1+i)^n} \) Price a stream of equal future payments today.
F / A Future value of a uniform series. \( F = A \cdot \dfrac{(1+i)^n - 1}{i} \) Project a savings plan or sinking fund.
A / P Annual equivalent of a present sum. \( A = P \cdot \dfrac{i\,(1+i)^n}{(1+i)^n - 1} \) Convert a loan into level payments.
A / F Annual equivalent of a future sum. \( A = F \cdot \dfrac{i}{(1+i)^n - 1} \) Set aside equal amounts to hit a target.

P/A and A/P are reciprocals; F/A and A/F are reciprocals. Memorise one of each pair.

§ II · Uniform series09 / 31
Read the table top-to-bottom. For each factor, give a concrete instance students recognise: P/A — pricing a 30-year mortgage to its lump sum today. F/A — projecting a 401(k) at retirement from monthly contributions. A/P — your monthly mortgage payment, given the principal. A/F — saving for a child's college tuition due in 18 years. Stress the reciprocal pairs. In an exam, knowing one of each pair lets you derive the other; no need to memorise four formulas. ~5 minutes.
§ IIWhere it comes from 10
A 60-second derivation

P/A is just a geometric series
in a smart disguise.

Discount each of the n equal payments of A back to today:

Step 1 — sum of discounted payments
\( P = \displaystyle\sum_{t=1}^{n} \dfrac{A}{(1+i)^t} \)
Step 2 — factor and sum the geometric series
\( P = A \cdot \dfrac{1 - (1+i)^{-n}}{i} = A \cdot \dfrac{(1+i)^n - 1}{i\,(1+i)^n} \)

Why this matters

  • The formula is not arbitrary — it's the closed form of n single-sum PVs.
  • As n → ∞, the factor collapses to 1/i — the price of a perpetuity.
  • As i → 0, the factor collapses to n — undiscounted total.

Both limits are useful sanity checks on any spreadsheet answer.

§ II · Uniform series10 / 31
Don't dwell on the algebra — show it, name it, move on. The pedagogical goal is to *demystify* the factor: it isn't a black box, it's a compressed sum of single-sum PVs from Lecture 3. Two limits are gold: • i → 0: factor → n. A perfectly flat world; the stream is just n payments of A. • n → ∞: factor → 1/i. A perpetuity. Worth knowing — the cost of a $100/year subscription forever at 5% is $2,000. These limits are exam-favourite sanity checks. ~3 minutes.
§ IIWorked example · P/A 11
Pricing a stream — P/A

What is a $1,500/month rental promise worth today?

A landlord receives $1,500 at the end of each month for the next five years (60 months). The opportunity cost of capital is 9% per year, compounded monthly — so i = 0.75% per month.

Apply P/A
\( P = 1500 \cdot \dfrac{(1.0075)^{60} - 1}{0.0075 \cdot (1.0075)^{60}} \)
\( P \approx 1500 \cdot 48.173 \approx \$72{,}260 \)

What the answer tells you

  • The undiscounted total is $90,000 (60 × $1,500) — the time-value gap is $17.7K, about 20%.
  • If a buyer offers $70K cash today to take over the lease, the landlord should refuse — the stream is worth more.
  • At i = 0 (no time-value), the same stream is worth $90K; the gap is entirely the cost of waiting.

Always check that i and the period of A are in the same unit. Mixing kills the answer.

§ II · Uniform series11 / 31
Walk the numbers slowly. The key teaching move: emphasise the unit match. The rent is monthly; we converted the annual 9% to a monthly 0.75% before applying the factor. Many wrong answers come from plugging an annual i into a formula with monthly A. Anchor the result: $72K today is the SAME as $1.5K/month for 60 months, given 9%/yr. Both numbers represent the same wealth. ~5 minutes.
§ IIWorked example · A/P 12
Sizing a payment — A/P

Lump-sum server vs. monthly cloud bill.

A team can buy a server outright for $60,000, or pay a hyperscaler monthly for the same compute. At 9%/yr over a 3-year life, what monthly bill would make the two equivalent?

A/P at i = 0.75% per month, n = 36
\( A = 60{,}000 \cdot \dfrac{0.0075 \cdot (1.0075)^{36}}{(1.0075)^{36} - 1} \)
\( A \approx 60{,}000 \cdot 0.03180 \approx \$1{,}908 \,/\, \text{month} \)

How to read the result

Any cloud bill below $1,908/month makes leasing cheaper than owning, ignoring everything else (depreciation, maintenance, salvage).

A real comparison would add: power, ops, scaling head-room, optionality — we will layer those in Lecture 11 (Build / Buy / Reuse).

§ II · Uniform series12 / 31
Connect this to Lecture 1's build-vs-buy teaser and to Lecture 11. Right now we're just sizing the *equivalent* monthly payment. That's only the first input into the full build-vs-buy decision. Optional extension if time permits: ask the class to compute the equivalent annual amount (use i=9% directly, n=3): A ≈ $23,700/yr. Same wealth, different units. ~4 minutes.

When the same number

Maintenance costs that creep up by $5K every year are the canonical arithmetic gradient.

§ III · Arithmetic gradientLecture Four
Brief transition. The world rarely gives you perfectly flat streams. The next two parts cover the two ways streams TILT — linearly (arithmetic) or proportionally (geometric). ~30 seconds.
§ IIIThe shape 14
A constant additive step

An arithmetic gradient adds G every period.

A pure arithmetic gradient · base 0 · step G

A pure arithmetic gradient starts at zero at period 1, grows by a constant G each period, and is rarely a stream you actually receive in isolation — almost always you see it added to a uniform series base A1.

§ III · Arithmetic gradient14 / 31
Two teaching moves: (1) The pure gradient *starts at zero* at t=1, not at G. This trips everyone up — including me — at first. The first cash flow is at t=2 with value G. The factor P/G is built assuming this timing. (2) Real streams are usually a uniform A *plus* a gradient G. We will decompose one in Part VI. ~3 minutes.
§ IIIP/G and A/G 15
Two factors, two uses

Price the tilt — or convert it to an equivalent uniform.

P / G — present value of a pure arithmetic gradient
\( P_G = G \cdot \dfrac{(1+i)^n - i\,n - 1}{i^{2}\,(1+i)^n} \)
A / G — equivalent uniform-series amount of a gradient
\( A_G = G \cdot \left[\,\dfrac{1}{i} - \dfrac{n}{(1+i)^n - 1}\,\right] \)
"A flat series of AG per period is worth the same as a gradient stepping by G."

For a stream that starts at A1 at period 1 and grows by G per period, the PV is the sum of two pieces: a uniform series of A1 (use P/A) plus a pure gradient of G (use P/G).

§ III · Arithmetic gradient15 / 31
Don't ask the class to memorise P/G. Tell them: factor tables exist for a reason — look them up. What they MUST remember is the decomposition rule on the last line: an "increasing arithmetic stream" = "flat base" + "pure gradient." Combine P/A and P/G. A/G is most useful when comparing a gradient to a competing flat contract: convert the gradient to its equivalent uniform, then compare apples to apples. ~4 minutes.
§ IIIWorked example 16
Rising maintenance cost

Year 1: $20K. Then $5K more every year, for six years.

A legacy system costs $20K to maintain in year 1, and that cost grows by $5K per year through year 6. At i = 8%, what is the PV of the maintenance burden?

Decompose: uniform $20K base + pure gradient G = $5K
\( P = 20K \cdot (P/A, 8\%, 6) + 5K \cdot (P/G, 8\%, 6) \)
\( P \approx 20K \cdot 4.6229 + 5K \cdot 10.523 \)
\( P \approx \$92{,}460 + \$52{,}615 \approx \$145{,}080 \)

Why this matters for software economics

Almost every long-lived system has a creeping maintenance gradient — the codebase ages, dependencies decay, the team rotates. Quoting only year-1 cost ($20K) understates the lifetime burden by more than seven times.

A 60% increase in year-1 cost over six years is roughly an 8%/yr arithmetic gradient on $20K base — not unusual in practice.

§ III · Arithmetic gradient16 / 31
Take the calculation step by step. Walk students through the decomposition: "the stream is 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45 — that's a flat 20 plus a pure gradient that goes 0, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25." Stress the policy implication: if a vendor quotes only year-1 maintenance, the long-term burden is much larger. This is exactly the kind of analysis that earns engineers a seat in budget meetings. ~5 minutes.

When the same number

Inflation, SaaS price escalators, salary bands, AI token spend — the modern economy runs on geometric gradients.

§ IV · Geometric gradientLecture Four
Transition. If the arithmetic gradient is the gentle linear creep, the geometric gradient is the compounding ramp — and almost everything in the modern software business is geometric. Cloud cost, headcount, token consumption, ARR. ~30 seconds.
§ IVThe shape 18
A constant proportional step

A geometric gradient grows by a constant factor (1+g).

Geometric gradient · base A1 · rate g per period

In a geometric series, each period's cash flow is (1+g) times the previous. g > 0 is a ramp; g < 0 is a decay (declining licence renewal, for example).

§ IV · Geometric gradient18 / 31
Contrast visually with the arithmetic gradient from two slides ago. Arithmetic adds the SAME AMOUNT each period; geometric multiplies by the SAME FACTOR. The geometric one grows much faster for large n — the classic exponential outpaces the classic linear. Negative g matters too: declining maintenance cost (because the system gets retired piece by piece) is a geometric DECAY. The same formula handles both signs. ~3 minutes.
§ IVClosed form 19
One factor — two cases

One formula, two regimes: i ≠ g and i = g.

Case 1 — i ≠ g
\( P = A_1 \cdot \dfrac{1 - \left(\dfrac{1+g}{1+i}\right)^{n}}{i - g} \)
Case 2 — i = g (degenerate)
\( P = A_1 \cdot \dfrac{n}{1+i} \)
The growth exactly cancels the discount: each year's PV is the same constant.
Mnemonic: define i* = (i − g)/(1 + g), then a geometric stream with growth g and rate i is just a flat stream with rate i*. P/A with i* gives the answer.
§ IV · Geometric gradient19 / 31
The "i*" mnemonic is the most useful single trick on the slide. It reduces the geometric case to a uniform-series case students already know. Especially valuable in an exam — they only need P/A in muscle memory. For Case 2 (i = g), stress that this is a special case to know exists, not one they will see often. But when it occurs (e.g., growth ~ inflation baked into discount rate), the limit formula avoids 0/0. ~4 minutes.
§ IVWorked example · SaaS 20
The syllabus problem

A SaaS subscription growing 10% per year, for five years.

Year-1 payment $24,000; annual price escalator g = 10%; discount rate i = 9%; term n = 5. What is the PV of the five-year payment stream?

Apply Case 1 — i ≠ g
\( P = 24{,}000 \cdot \dfrac{1 - \left(\dfrac{1.10}{1.09}\right)^{5}}{0.09 - 0.10} \)
\( P = 24{,}000 \cdot \dfrac{1 - 1.0462}{-0.01} = 24{,}000 \cdot 4.6243 \)
\( P \approx \$110{,}983 \)

Sanity check — the i* trick

Effective rate i* = (0.09 − 0.10) / 1.10 ≈ −0.91%. P/A factor for n = 5 at i* ≈ −0.91% ≈ 5.139; times $24K × (1/1.10) ≈ $112K. Close to the closed form, modulo rounding.

Vendor's framing vs. customer's truth

The vendor quotes "$24K with a modest 10% annual increase" — the customer commits to a stream whose undiscounted total is $146.5K, and whose PV at 9% is $111K. Both numbers are real, and only the diagram reveals the gap.

§ IV · Geometric gradient20 / 31
This is the syllabus problem — students should leave today able to solve it cold. Walk it slowly. Note specifically: (1) i and g are both *annual*; periods match. Good. (2) g > i here, so the numerator term (1.10/1.09)^5 > 1, hence the numerator is negative; divided by (0.09 - 0.10) = -0.01, also negative; quotient is positive. Sign checks matter. (3) Result $110.98K is BELOW the undiscounted total of 24 + 26.4 + 29.04 + 31.94 + 35.14 = $146.5K. The gap ($35.5K) is the time-value of waiting up to 5 years for the later (larger) payments. Ask: "Would you sign this contract? What additional facts would you want before you do?" ~6 minutes.

Two streams are equivalent

Every financial decision in this course is, at root, an equivalence statement.

§ V · EquivalenceLecture Four
This is the conceptual heart of the lecture. The mechanics of P/A, P/G etc. exist so that we can speak about equivalence rigorously. Tell the class: when we say "this loan is worth $X today" or "the NPV of this project is positive" — every such statement is a claim of equivalence. ~30 seconds.
§ VDefinition 22
The central principle

Equivalence is what gives "worth the same" a precise meaning.

Two cash-flow streams X and Y are equivalent at interest rate i if

\( PV_i(X) = PV_i(Y) \)

Equivalence is — in this strong sense — the only meaningful definition of "same value" between two streams that occur at different times.

Three properties

  • Rate-dependent. Equivalence at 5% is not equivalence at 10%.
  • Symmetric & transitive. If X ≡ Y and Y ≡ Z (at the same i), then X ≡ Z.
  • Additive. If X1 ≡ Y1 and X2 ≡ Y2, then X1+X2 ≡ Y1+Y2.

Additivity is what makes decomposition (Part VI) work.

§ V · Equivalence22 / 31
Spend time on the rate-dependence point. A common student error is to think equivalence is a property of the streams alone. It isn't — it's a three-way relation: stream X, stream Y, AND rate i. Change i and equivalence can flip. Additivity is the mathematical justification for what we'll do in Part VI: split any messy real stream into clean shapes, price each, add them up. ~4 minutes.

§ VIThe toolkit 25
Every cash flow is a sum of these four

Four shapes, all the financial world.

Single sum

One payment, one period. Up-front investment, salvage value, one-time bonus.

Factor: P/F, F/P

Uniform series

The same amount, every period. Subscriptions, loan payments, salaries.

Factor: P/A, A/P, F/A, A/F

Arithmetic gradient

Constant additive step. Maintenance creep, ramping headcount, depreciating savings.

Factor: P/G, A/G

Geometric gradient

Constant proportional step. SaaS escalators, inflation, exponential growth or decay.

Factor: closed-form (1+g)/(1+i)

Decomposition rule — by additivity of equivalence: split the stream into clean pieces · price each at the same i · sum the PVs.

An enterprise software contract, decomposed

§ VI · Composition26 / 31
Walk the table row by row. Show: Row 1: outflow at t=0, no discounting needed, −$30K. Row 2: geometric gradient. Use the i* trick: i* = (0.09 - 0.06) / 1.06 ≈ 2.83%. P/A at i*=2.83%, n=5 ≈ 4.5733.
