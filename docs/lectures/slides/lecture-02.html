Part OneWhy cost? 03
Part — One
I

A cost concept is a lens,
not a line in a spreadsheet.

"The same dollar can be relevant or irrelevant to a decision — depending only on which way you look at it."

§ I · Cost as lensLecture Two
Tell the class the central claim of the lecture: a cost concept is not bookkeeping; it is a question you ask money about. The same $100 cloud bill can be a "fixed cost", a "sunk cost", an "opportunity cost", or an "opex line" — depending on the decision in front of you. Engineers who don't know the vocabulary make decisions on the wrong cost. That's the entire pedagogical motivation. ~1 minute.
§ ISame dollar, four lenses 04
A motivating puzzle

One $100 line — four different stories.

Your team pays $100/month for a code-search SaaS. Depending on the question, that single line item changes character.

The question we're askingWhat kind of cost is the $100?Decision consequence
Should we keep using it?A recurring, variable-with-headcount cost.Compare to value delivered each month.
Should we cancel because we paid annually?The pre-paid year is a sunk cost.Ignore it — decide on remaining months only.
Should we hire a contractor to replace it?The $100 is the opportunity cost of replacement.Build alternative only if it beats $100 + risk.
How should accounting record it?An operating expense (opex), not capex.Affects margin in the quarter it is paid.
The number is identical in every row. Only the question changes.
§ I · Cost as lens04 / 25
Walk row by row. For each, ask "what would change about the decision if we used the wrong lens?" Some answers: Row 1 → If we treat it as fixed, we never re-evaluate value-per-seat. Row 2 → If we count the pre-paid year, we'll keep using a bad tool because "we already paid". This is the sunk-cost trap. Row 3 → If we compare contractor cost only to the $100 and ignore quality/risk, we'll under-cost the alternative. Row 4 → Capex vs opex changes the quarterly P&L, not the cash flow, but it changes how the finance team reacts. ~4 minutes.

Part — Two
II

The taxonomy — four axes,
every software dollar has
a place on each.

Fixed or variable · direct or indirect · recurring or non-recurring · capital or operating.

§ II · TaxonomyLecture Two
Frame the next four slides as "the four axes". Every cost in a software project has a coordinate on each. A junior engineer typically knows none of these; a senior engineer can place any line item in seconds. That fluency is the goal. ~30 seconds.
§ IIAxis 1 — fixed vs variable 07
Axis 1 of 4 — does the cost move with volume?

Fixed pays the floor, variable pays per use.

Fixed

Does not change as volume changes — at least within the relevant range.

e.g. reserved server lease · team salaries · annual licence.

Variable

Scales (often linearly) with units of output, users, or load.

e.g. per-request inference cost · per-GB egress · token spend · seat-based SaaS.

"Fixed" is fixed only inside a range. A server is fixed until you outgrow it; a salary is fixed until you hire one more engineer.
§ II · Taxonomy07 / 25
Walk the chart slowly. Three lines: the flat dashed grey is FIXED; the indigo slope is VARIABLE; the crimson is their sum, TOTAL COST. Make sure students notice: at zero volume, you still pay the fixed cost. Anecdote: a startup that "shut down" a service to save money but kept paying $40K/month for the reserved Kubernetes cluster because no one owned the bill. Fixed costs are sticky — that's the engineering lesson. Mention the "relevant range" idea: nothing is truly fixed forever. ~5 minutes.
§ IIAxes 2 & 3 08
Axes 2 & 3 of 4 — attribution & recurrence

Can we trace it? Will it come back?

Direct vs Indirect

A direct cost can be traced to one project or product without allocation. An indirect cost serves many and must be split.

DirectSalary of an engineer working only on Project X.
DirectThe cloud account dedicated to Project X.
IndirectHR, IT support, the office lease, the CI runner pool.
IndirectThe CTO's time — shared across every project.

Indirect costs are often called overhead and applied as a percentage on top of direct cost.

Recurring vs Non-recurring

A non-recurring cost is paid once. A recurring cost returns every month, quarter, or year for the life of the system.

Non-recurringInitial data migration · architecture spike.
Non-recurringOne-time hardware purchase · build setup.
RecurringMonthly SaaS bill · per-token API cost.
RecurringOn-call rota · annual compliance audit.

Engineers under-count recurring; finance under-counts non-recurring. Both biases bite.

§ II · Taxonomy08 / 25
For DIRECT/INDIRECT — students often miss that the CTO's time is a real cost. When the CTO spends two days on your project, the company is paying for it; it just doesn't appear on your project's cost line. This is why companies allocate overhead — to make sure indirect costs are recovered. For RECURRING/NON-RECURRING — give the canonical anecdote: a team that celebrated shipping a feature "for $40K" and then paid $25K/year forever in cloud costs they hadn't planned. Year 3, the recurring cost has already eclipsed the build cost. Recurring is the silent killer. ~6 minutes.
§ IIAxis 4 — capex vs opex 09
Axis 4 of 4 — accounting treatment

Capital builds the asset. Operating runs it.

 Capital Expenditure (CapEx)Operating Expenditure (OpEx)
What it isMoney spent to acquire a long-lived asset.Money spent to run the business this period.
Software examplesServer purchase · perpetual licence · self-built platform.Cloud bill · SaaS subscription · per-token API spend.
AccountingCapitalised and depreciated over useful life.Expensed in the period incurred — hits margin directly.
Cash flowBig up-front outflow; smaller drag thereafter.Smaller, steady, predictable outflow.
Strategic effectBuilds an owned asset; cheaper at scale; less flexible.No asset on the balance sheet; flexible; cheaper at low scale.
The 2010s "shift from CapEx to OpEx" is exactly the move from data centres to the cloud — same workload, different accounting, different freedom to scale.
§ II · Taxonomy09 / 25
Anchor this to a story students know: every cloud-migration debate of the last 15 years has been a CapEx→OpEx debate, even if the engineers in the room didn't know the terms. The trade-off: capex is cheaper at scale but inflexible; opex is more expensive per-unit but you can turn it off tomorrow. Many AI workloads today face the same fork: do we buy GPUs (capex) or rent them (opex)? Lecture 13 returns to this in token economics. ~5 minutes.

Part — Three
III

The four costs that behave
differently in a decision —
and the rule for which to count.

Opportunity · sunk · marginal · average.

§ III · BehaviouralLecture Two
Frame: the previous part was *what kind* of cost. This part is *how does the cost behave in a decision.* It's a smaller set — four concepts — but each one is responsible for a famous engineering mistake. Tell students by the end of this section they should be able to recognise each mistake in the wild. ~30 seconds.
§ IIIOpportunity cost 11
Concept 1 of 4

Opportunity cost — the road not taken.

The value of the next best alternative you gave up by choosing what you chose.

It is almost never on an invoice — so it is almost never in the spreadsheet — so it is almost never in the decision.

Formula, in spirit: true cost = cash cost + opportunity cost

A small example

Your three best engineers spend Q3 rewriting the build system.

  • Cash cost of the rewrite: $0 — they're salaried.
  • Opportunity cost: the customer feature those three engineers didn't ship.
  • If that feature would have generated $300K, the rewrite cost $300K — not $0.

Engineers who say "internal work is free" are silently making the largest economic error in the field.

§ III · Behavioural11 / 25
This is the single most under-counted cost in tech. Hammer it. Stories to tell: • The team that spent six months on an internal tooling project that paid back $50K/yr in saved engineer time — but the engineers could have built a customer feature worth $500K/yr in the same months. • The CEO who treats founder time as "free" and personally fixes bugs while a contractor would cost $200/hr. The point: "free" never means free. It means the cost isn't on an invoice. Senior engineers learn to see invisible costs; that's part of what makes them senior. ~5 minutes.
§ IIISunk cost 12
Concept 2 of 4

Sunk cost — money already spent, decisions already over.

A sunk cost has already been incurred and cannot be recovered, regardless of which option you now choose.

It is therefore irrelevant to the decision in front of you. Rationally, you must ignore it.

In practice, humans (and especially teams that championed the original investment) cannot ignore it. This is the sunk-cost fallacy.

Three software sunk-cost traps

  • Six-month migration is 80% done; the new path is worse than where we started — but "we've come too far to turn back."
  • An annual SaaS subscription was paid in January; we keep forcing the team to use a tool nobody likes "to get our money's worth."
  • Three engineers wrote a custom framework no one else uses; we maintain it forever rather than admitting it was a wrong turn.

The healthy phrase: "What would we decide if we were arriving today, with no history?"

§ III · Behavioural12 / 25
The sunk-cost fallacy is universal — every student has done it. Ask for a show of hands: "Have you ever finished a Netflix show you weren't enjoying because you'd watched four episodes?" Most hands go up. That is the sunk-cost fallacy in miniature. The professional version is more expensive. Migrations, frameworks, partnerships — all classic traps. Teach the cure: a literal mental phrase. "If we were arriving today, with no history, would we choose this path?" If the answer is no, the sunk cost is dragging you. Walk away. ~5 minutes.
§ IIIMarginal vs average 13
Concepts 3 & 4 of 4

The next unit costs less
(or more) than the average.

Marginal cost

The cost of one more unit — the next request, the next inference, the next user, the next feature.

Average cost

Total cost divided by total units. Easy to compute, easy to mislead with.

For most decisions in software, the question is marginal, not average. "Should we accept one more API request?" is a marginal question; "What does our service cost per user on average?" is a reporting question.
§ III · Behavioural13 / 25
The cost curves both U-shape because: at low volume, fixed costs are spread over few units (average is high); at high volume you hit capacity limits, congestion, and on-call overhead (marginal climbs). Sweet spot is where marginal cost crosses average — the classical "optimal scale" insight. Software-specific intuition: serverless API has near-zero marginal cost at low scale, then a sharp climb when you hit per-request pricing tiers. LLM inference has marginal cost dominated by tokens — easy to compute, often ignored. Lecture 13 will return to this. ~5 minutes.
§ IIIDecision rule 14
The single rule that makes the taxonomy useful

In any decision, count only
the future and the different.

The taxonomy on its own is just labels. The rule below is what turns labels into better choices.

Count these — Decision-relevant
  • Future costs that have not yet been incurred.
  • Differential costs that differ between the alternatives.
  • Opportunity costs — the value of what you give up.
  • Cash-flow consequences, including risk.
Ignore these — Irrelevant to this decision
  • Sunk costs — already spent, unrecoverable.
  • Common costs that are identical across alternatives.
  • Allocated overhead that will be paid either way.
  • Accounting artefacts with no cash consequence.
§ III · Behavioural14 / 25
This is the single most important slide in the lecture. Pause on it. Tell the class: if you internalise nothing else today, internalise this. The vocabulary of cost types is so they can EXECUTE this rule — recognise "this is sunk, ignore it"; "this is differential, count it"; "this is allocated, irrelevant to this decision." Most bad project decisions are rule violations: someone counted a sunk cost, or someone ignored an opportunity cost, or someone compared total cost when they should have compared incremental cost. ~5 minutes.

§ IV · LifecycleLecture Two
The big reveal of this part: development is the small slice; everything after launch is the big slice. Most engineers spend most of their time thinking about the small slice. Boehm's most cited finding is that maintenance dominates lifetime cost — often 60-80%. ~30 seconds.
§ IVFive phases 16
The shape of a software lifetime

Five phases — and the iceberg.

I
Acquisition
Specification, vendor selection, contract, procurement.
II
Development
Design, build, test, deploy. The phase engineers see most.
III
Operation
Run-time hosting, support, on-call, observability.
IV
Maintenance
Bug fixes, evolution, dependency updates, security patches.
V
Retirement
Data migration, sunsetting, archival, decommissioning.
Development~ 20 %
Launch
Operation + Maintenance≈ 80 %
+ Evolution + Retirement

Boehm's iceberg

Most engineering culture, most planning meetings, and most cost spreadsheets concern themselves with the visible 20% — design and build.

Boehm (1981) found that 60–80% of lifetime cost lives below the waterline: in operation, maintenance, and evolution. Modern AI-era systems are not exempt — and often worse.

A decision that lowers build cost 30% but raises maintenance cost 30% almost always makes the project more expensive.
§ IV · Lifecycle16 / 25
Walk the timeline left to right. Hover slightly on phase IV (Maintenance) — that's the one engineers under-spend on emotionally. Then move to the iceberg. The numbers are well-cited but vary by domain — some studies (Lientz & Swanson) put maintenance at 60%; others as high as 90% for long-lived enterprise systems. The consistent story: development is the tip, not the body. Implication for decisions: ANY decision made during build that shifts cost into ops/maintenance can wipe out apparent build savings. This is the rationale for TCO thinking — the next section. ~7 minutes.

§ V · TCO18 / 25
Walk the bars left to right. Highlight three things: (1) Year 0 (the "build") is dwarfed by every operating year. (2) Tokens — a line that did not exist in Boehm's 1981 canon — grow every year as the team uses the tool more. They are recurring, variable-with-usage costs. Build a five-year TCO worksheet.

Worksheet — cost lines to consider (don't restrict yourself to these)

Build & setup

  • Engineer time
  • Architecture / spike
  • Initial data migration
  • Security review
  • UX research

Operate & maintain

  • SaaS / licence fees
  • Cloud / infra
  • On-call rota
  • Observability tooling
  • Annual security audit

AI-era & retire

  • LLM tokens (in/out/cache)
  • Vector DB · embeddings
  • GPU hours (if any)

  • Retirement / archive
For each line, mark it on three axes: fixed / variable · recurring / one-time · direct / indirect. Then circle anything that would be different between two realistic alternatives.
§ V · Exercise19 / 25
Set the exercise carefully. • Pairs — not solo, not teams of four. Pairs talk most. • 10 minutes hard timer; warn at 5 and 1 minutes left. • At the end, ask three pairs to share *the cost line most teams forget*. Almost always: retirement, security audit, or LLM tokens. Move around the room — listen for the word "free." Whenever you hear it, ask: "What's the opportunity cost?" After 10 minutes of pair work + 5 of share-back = 15 minutes total. ~15 minutes.

§ VI · Modern ledger21 / 25
Highlight the SHADED rows — those are the lines that did not exist in Boehm's textbook. Five out of eight modern lines are new in the AI era.
